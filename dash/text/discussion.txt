Our analysis reveals that rideshare trips are almost universally faster than their public transit counterparts. While rideshare is significantly more expensive, with nearly all neighborhood corridors exceeding the $2.50 CTA fare and high-cost routes surpassing $70, the average user appears to be making a rational choice regarding their time, if not their money. Ultimately, the time savings threshold for choosing rideshare over transit likely scales inversely with personal income.

A significant portion of the most expensive and longest trips are concentrated around O’Hare International Airport and, to a lesser extent, Midway. These hubs remain outliers in terms of demand, cost, and distance. In addition to the airports, the Loop and its immediate neighbors (such as River North and the West Loop) are the most frequent pickup locations.

Interestingly, many of the most popular corridors maintain a relatively low "transit penalty." This indicates that many Chicagoans opt for rideshare even when the transit alternative is not significantly longer (less than twice as long). While our analysis identifies several target corridors for service improvements, it also suggests that efficiency alone may not shift behavior for everyone. However, localized service improvements remain vital; as research suggests, high-quality service in one part of the network can create a "halo effect" that improves public perception and ridership for the entire system.

In exploring our interactive map, we observed that many rides with the highest transit penalties occur between adjacent neighborhoods. This suggests that the CTA’s current spoke-and-hub model may not sufficiently serve shorter, lateral trips. Furthermore, a large percentage of rideshare trips are under one mile, highlighting the "last-mile problem." These micro-trips might be more effectively replaced by expanded pedestrian and bike infrastructure rather than new fixed transit routes.

Finally, we observed that neighborhoods with the poorest transit alternatives for their top five destinations are often those already underserved in other capacities. These neighborhoods are predominantly located on the South and West Sides, including Chatham, Englewood, and Little Village.
